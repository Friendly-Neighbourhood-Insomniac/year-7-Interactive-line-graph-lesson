Learning Objectives

  • Interpret and draw line graphs.

1. Introduction to Line Graphs

Line graphs are powerful tools used to display data that changes over time. They help us see trends, patterns, and make predictions about future outcomes. In this lesson, we'll learn how to interpret and draw line graphs accurately, and how to identify misleading graphs.

2. Key Terms Defined

  • Line Graph: A type of chart used to show information that changes over time. It displays data points connected by straight line segments.
  • Misleading: Providing the wrong idea or impression, often by omitting important information or manipulating visual elements.

3. Common Errors and Misconceptions

3.1 Starting Axis at Zero Instead of Breaking It

Students might start the vertical axis at zero even when data values are far from zero. This can compress the graph and make significant changes appear minor.

Tip: Use a break in the axis or adjust the starting point to focus on the relevant data range.

3.2 Choosing Inappropriate Scales

Using scales with awkward intervals, like steps of 3, can make it difficult to read values accurately.

Tip: Choose scales that make it easy to plot points and read values, such as increments of 1, 2, 5, or 10.

4. Main Teaching Concepts

4.1 Plotting Two Graphs with Different Vertical Axes

Let's consider a battery's voltage over time. We'll plot two graphs showing the voltage drop of a battery over 8 hours.

Example 1: Battery Voltage Graphs

Graph A: Vertical axis starts at 0V.

Graph B: Vertical axis starts at 8V.

Both graphs plot the same data, but Graph B appears to show a more dramatic drop in voltage because of the compressed vertical scale.

Question: Which graph exaggerates the drop in voltage?

Answer: Graph B exaggerates the drop because it starts at 8V, making the decline appear steeper.

4.2 Comparing Two Batteries

Now, let's compare two different batteries: the "PowerMax" and the "Lasar" battery. We'll plot both on the same graph using different colors or line styles and include a key.

Example 2: Battery Comparison

Data:

  • PowerMax Battery: Voltage drops from 9V to 6V over 8 hours.
  • Lasar Battery: Voltage drops from 9V to 5V over 8 hours.

Questions:

  1. Which battery lost its charge fastest in the first two hours?
  2. Which battery lost its charge fastest in the last two hours?
  3. How could you draw the graph for the Lasar battery so that its voltage drop appears faster?

Answers:

  1. The Lasar battery lost its charge fastest in the first two hours.
  2. In the last two hours, both batteries lost charge at the same rate.
  3. By numbering the vertical axis from 7V instead of 0V, making the drop appear steeper.

4.3 Identifying Misleading Graphs

A biology student recorded the number of bacteria in a Petri dish every hour and drew a line graph. However, his graph was misleading because the vertical scale did not increase in equal steps.

Example 3: Misleading Bacteria Growth Graph

Issue: The vertical axis increments were inconsistent, distorting the representation of bacterial growth.

Corrected Graph: When plotted with equal increments, the graph accurately shows the growth pattern.

Question: Do you think the corrected graph is a good model for the next few weeks?

Answer: No, because physical limitations like food source and space in the Petri dish will affect growth.

4.4 Discussing Best and Worst Scenarios

Line graphs can be manipulated to show best or worst-case scenarios by adjusting scales and starting points.

Discussion:

Question: How can line graphs show the worst and best scenarios?

Answer: By changing the scale or starting point of the axes, or by omitting data points, graphs can exaggerate or minimize trends to present data more positively or negatively.

5. Why Learn This?

Line graphs are essential tools for predicting future outcomes based on current data trends. For example, they can illustrate how recycling efforts reduce landfill waste over time, helping policymakers make informed decisions.

Real-Life Application: Landfill Reduction

By plotting the amount of waste sent to landfills over several years, a line graph can show the effectiveness of recycling programs. This visual representation helps communities understand the impact of their actions and encourages continued efforts.

6. Practice Questions

Question 1

A student recorded the temperature at noon each day for a week: 20°C, 22°C, 21°C, 19°C, 23°C, 25°C, and 24°C. Draw a line graph to represent this data.

Question 2

Explain why starting the vertical axis at 15°C instead of 0°C might be misleading in representing the temperature data from Question 1.

Question 3

A company shows a line graph of their profits over five years but uses unequal intervals on the horizontal axis. How could this be misleading?

Question 4

Create a line graph using the following data on recycling rates (in tons) over four years: Year 1 - 500, Year 2 - 750, Year 3 - 1000, Year 4 - 1250. Choose an appropriate scale.

Question 5

Discuss how adjusting the scale on a graph can change the viewer's perception of the data trends.

7. Answers to Practice Questions

Question 1 Answer

Draw a line graph with the horizontal axis representing days of the week and the vertical axis representing temperature in °C. Plot the points and connect them with straight lines.

Question 2 Answer

Starting the vertical axis at 15°C exaggerates the fluctuations in temperature, making small changes appear more significant than they are.

Question 3 Answer

Unequal intervals on the horizontal axis can distort the perception of time between data points, misleading viewers about the rate of profit increase or decrease.

Question 4 Answer

Choose a vertical scale that increments evenly, such as steps of 250 tons, ranging from 0 to 1300 tons. Plot the years on the horizontal axis and draw the line graph accordingly.

Question 5 Answer

Adjusting the scale can either compress or stretch the graph, making trends appear less or more dramatic. This can influence how the data is interpreted, potentially leading to misconceptions.
